I NT RO DU CT ION

Digital deliverable services have grown fastest among all international trade in goods and services,
creating benefits for the global economy. The tradability of digital services relies heavily on the
ability of companies to transfer data across borders (WTO, 2018). In turn, the capacity to move
data across countries by firms is affected by rules governing data. Yet, these rules are increasingly
fragmented across the globe. This article examines the digital trade consequences of such data
fragmentation.
Specifically, this article examines if sharing a similar regulatory model for cross-border data
transfers and a data protection law between trading partners is associated with trade in digital services. Rules governing the cross-border transfer of personal data follow roughly three
different regulatory models, each based on different features: one based on open transfers of
data, a second based on conditional transfers with safeguards in place, and a third based on
government-controlled transfers. This regulatory divergence is likely to distort trade in digital services across the global economy, which we study. We also study the interaction of these models
with the presence of a comprehensive data protection law between country-pairs. Such a policy
might, on the one the hand, create compliance costs for businesses, but on the other, it can raise
trust for consumers.
To shed light on our research question, we perform a structural gravity model in which we
assess the digital services trade effects for partner-countries that share one of the three data
models.
Before we perform our gravity model, we first identify the governance models for personal data
that countries have applied over the years for 143 countries. For each country, we assess the model
implemented with regards to the cross-border transfers of personal data. We show that there are
large differences between and within regions of the world. The open model, often associated with
the U.S. approach, is dominant in North America, whereas the control model followed by China
is prevalent in East Asia. The conditional model, which in large part is driven by the EU, has
become widely used in Latin America, Oceania, and to some extent in Africa.
In a second step, we identify which countries have implemented comprehensive regulations
regarding the protection of personal data. While these obligations may increase the costs of doing
business for firms, mainly in the form of compliance costs, they nonetheless could instil trust in
the digital economy. In addition, as most countries have implemented a domestic regime of data
protection inspired by the European Union model (the so-called Brussels-effect), the presence of
a comprehensive data protection law could have a positive impact on trade by lowering regulatory
heterogeneity. Therefore, in this article, we also examine how such regulations of data protection
interact with each model for the cross-border transfer of data.

Our gravity model applies a state-of-the art approach in which we use stringent fixed effects
between country-pairs interacted over time. The reason we can apply such demanding method
is because our gravity model makes use of a so-called difference-in-difference component (DiD)
in which we make a distinction between digital versus non-digital services. Our binary indicator
of whether a sector is classified as a digital service or not is interacted with our main variables of
interest, namely whether countries share one of the three data models. A significant result on any
of these variables of interest would indicate that having a data model in common affects countries
trading patterns towards more or less digital services trade between them.
The basic premise of such empirical approach is illustrated in Figure 1 which shows three
staggered DiD plots of the normalized ratio of digital trade over non-digital trade, shown on the
vertical axis, for each data model, with the horizontal dashed line indicating its threshold. The
vertical axis plots the 5 years before and the 6 years after the introduction of a data model shared
by any two partner countries in our country sample (measured by the vertical dashed line). The
open model clearly shows an upward trend towards a higher ratio of digital over non-digital services, which has become significant for countries sharing this model after their starting point.
An opposing trend is visible for the conditional model with weak significance, whereas the control model no significant pattern can be revealed. Our gravity model allows us to estimate these
outcomes in a more structured setting.
This article contributes to several strands of the literature assessing the relationship between
cross-border data flows and international trade, from an economic and legal vantage point. To
start with the latter, Aaronson and Leblond (2018), Gao (2018), and Azmeh et al. (2019) describe
the existing data realms from a legal and political economy perspective, which are centred around
the three global actors, namely the U.S., EU, and China. We use this literature as a starting
point for our categorization to assess which model is implemented by 143 countries around the
world, as illustrated in Figure A1. We also identify whether each country has implemented a
comprehensive data protection framework, as illustrated in Figure A2.
We combine the legal side of this literature with recent empirical economic works assessing
the extent to which regulatory data policies affect international trade in digital services. Specifically, Ferracane and van der Marel (2021) examine how regulatory restrictions related to the
cross-border flow and domestic processing of data affect trade in digital services of countries
imposing them. Recent works by Sun and Trefler (2023) relates regulatory restrictions in data to
observed data streams. Other works examine the economic outcomes of privacy, such as Acquisti
et al. (2016). This article differs from these works by not taking the restrictive nature of regulations as a unit measure, but taking into account the wider regulatory framework for data (i.e.,
data model) that countries have implemented.
The results of our empirical analysis show that trading partners sharing the open model
exhibit positive trade correlations in digital services, whereas country-pairs sharing the conditional model show a negative one, in line with Figure 1. When our proxy for data protection is
interacted with each data model variable, the latter outcome gets muted: the negative effect of
sharing the conditional model is offset by the positive impact arising from sharing a comprehensive data protection law. Countries having such a protection framework in place are therefore
likely to pre-empt the negative cost effect of applying safeguard conditions for cross-border data
transfers. Yet, data protection does not have any meaningful outcome on countries sharing the
open model and shows a significant negative outcome for the control model, in line with our
expectations.
We believe our findings are relevant for policymakers, and especially relevant for developing countries given their potential to benefit from participating in digital services trade. Many

Early efforts to collect regulatory information on data can be found in Ferracane et al. (2018),

trade in digital services, as empirically shown in Ferracane et al. (2020), Ferracane and van der
Marel (2021) and Sun and Trefler (2023).
Insofar as these data restrictions are part of a broader governance framework, a separate
strand of the literature closer to our work identifies the defining features of the existing global
data realms in which these restrictions evolve. Aaronson and Leblond (2018) show that data
governance frameworks across the globe take shape in three distinct models, namely the ones
developed by the U.S., the EU, and China. The authors discuss data-related governance with
respect to trade policy, including a reference to the treatment of data privacy. Gao (2018) presents
the contrasting policy approaches between China and the US with respect to digital trade and
data, while Gao (2019) delves deeper into the defining features of the China model with regards
to data regulations.
Other recent works also make note of the differing global governance models for data. These
works either discuss the three data models in relation to the political economy of digital policies
in the US (Azmeh et al., 2019) or from the perspective of the WTO (Hodson, 2019; Sen, 2018). In
addition, works by Mattoo and Meltzer (2018) and Meltzer (2019) discuss the data governance
framework of the EU specifically and the potential for regulatory cooperation between the EU and
US. These authors also point to the distinction between restrictive policies inhibiting digital trade
and other associated regulatory policies related to data protection. More recently, Bradford (2019,
2023) has delved into the legal and technical aspects that categorize the three competing digital
governance models led by the EU, the U.S., and China.
This study contributes to this mainly legal literature by analyzing the digital trade impacts for
a wide set of countries that have implemented one of the three data models, as shown in Figure A1
and as will be discussed in the next section. The economic literature is silent on the potential
economic trade effects following the implementation of these regulatory governance models for
data. Only a few studies have analyzed at more aggregate level the potential economic effects of
one specific model only, namely the one of the EU, called the General Data Protection Regulation
(GDPR). Christensen et al. (2013) uses calibration techniques to evaluate the impact of the GDPR
proposal on small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and concludes that SMEs that use data
intensively are likely to incur substantial costs in complying with these new rules. Another study
by Bauer et al. (2013) uses a computable general equilibrium model to estimate the economic
impact of the GDPR and finds a reduction of trade between the EU and the rest of the world.
At a more general level, this article also contributes to a broader strand of the literature covering research on the economics of privacy, including the economic value and consequences of
protecting and disclosing personal data. For instance, Acquisti et al. (2016) provides a general
overview of the theoretical and empirical research on the economics of privacy and shows how

G LO BA L DATA MODE LS

We start by developing a taxonomy to identify the three governance models for cross-border transfer of personal data based on their specific features. We focus on cross-border transfers of personal
data given that this issue is the most relevant regulatory issue for digital technologies and trade
(Goldfarb & Trefler, 2018). Having this taxonomy, we then systematically categorize 143 countries
under one of the three data models. In addition, we also explore whether these countries have
implemented a comprehensive regime for data protection.
While personal data regulations diverge widely between countries, it is nonetheless possible
to identify three main approaches with distinctive characteristics: one based on open transfers
and processing of data, a second based on conditional data transfers, and a third based on controlled data transfers. These three models for data governance have become a reference for many
countries around the world when defining their data policies.3

3.1

Taxonomy

Table 1 summarizes the main features of the three data models.
The open model, led by the U.S., is characterized by the absence of restrictions on cross-border
data flows and the absence of a comprehensive framework for personal data protection that
applies to domestic data processing. With regards to cross-border data transfers, countries following the open model usually rely on a baseline set of privacy principles, leaving to companies the
flexibility to self-regulate on a voluntary basis. Under this model, firms usually remain accountable for how personal data is treated, including when it is transferred to a recipient in a third
country. However, several countries following this model simply lack any accountability on how
personal data is treated after it crosses borders.

The conditional model, pioneered by the EU, is characterized by the presence of certain conditions for the transfers and processing of personal data. Countries following this model take a
comprehensive and fundamental rights approach to data protection with preventative regulation.
Regarding cross-border data transfers, countries applying this model impose certain conditions to
be fulfilled ex-ante for the transfer of personal data across borders. These conditions can be quite
diverse, including the consent of the data subject, the use of specific legal mechanisms such as
binding corporate rules, the compliance with specific codes of conduct, or the requirement that
the recipient countries have a regime for data protection considered as ‘adequate’.
Finally, the third model is the one that China has implemented and is based on controlled
transfers and processing of data. This model is more common among countries where the concept
of the right to privacy is quite recent. Countries following this model tend to link data privacy
to cybersecurity, given that, generally, data regulation is elevated to a matter of national security
(e.g., see Gao, 2019). Countries following this model are characterized by extensive restrictions on
cross-border data transfers and by systematic control of personal data by national authorities. For
cross-border transfers of personal data, this model imposes strict restrictions, which include the
local processing of data or the ex-ante authorization by the government to transfer data abroad
following a security assessment.

3.2

Data protection

We also assess each country’s implementation of a comprehensive data protection regime. Such a
framework includes the consent of the data subject for data collection, extensive rights, including
the right to access, modify and delete data, and, in most cases, the establishment of data protection
authorities. While these obligations may increase the costs of doing business for firms, mainly in
compliance costs, these policies are also expected to instil trust in consumers with regard to the
digital economy.
In addition, as most countries have implemented a domestic regime of data protection inspired
by the European Union model (the so-called Brussels-effect), a comprehensive data protection
law could positively impact trade by lowering regulatory heterogeneity. When a country does not
implement such a regime, data subjects have limited rights regarding how their personal data is
handled. It is unusual that a country lacks any measures related to privacy, although this is still the
case in some least developed economies. When a comprehensive regime is lacking, it is common
for certain sensitive categories of data, such as in finance and health, to have sectoral rules on
data processing. In these cases, data protection is usually treated as a consumer protection right.

3.3

Global developments

The choice of countries to implement one of the three data models has dramatically changed over
the years. In 1990, most countries had an open model for data transfers, in part because many
countries did not have any data regulations yet in place (Figure 2). This has changed significantly
over the years. Following the introduction of the EU’s Data Protection Directive (DPD) in 1995,
many more countries have replaced the open model of data with a conditional regime for data
transfer, which usually went hand-in-hand with a comprehensive data protection law applied
domestically. In 2000, 80% of the countries in our sample still had an open data model in place,
which in 2022 was diminished to a share of 22%. In 2022, the conditional model represented

EMPIRICAL SPEC IFICAT ION, DATA, AND RESULTS

This section presents the baseline regression specification using the gravity model. In doing so,
we estimate whether countries sharing one of the three data models is correlated with greater or
lower trade in digital services compared to the benchmark of countries implementing a dissimilar
data model. The empirical strategy makes use of a binary variable between the exporting and
importing country, varying over time, which indicates whether country-pairs in our sample share
the same data model measured with a value equal to one. We assign a value of zero if they don’t.
Because of its dyadic nature, this indicator resembles many other conventional time-varying
bilateral gravity variables, such as whether countries share an FTA or are member of the WTO.
Yet, the way in which we categorize data frameworks as our variable of interest looks closer to
the indicator developed by La Porta et al. (2008), which measures whether countries share similar legal origins, and which is divided into a French, German, and Scandinavian legal origin for
countries around the world. The interpretation of our variable is therefore closer to this latter

4.2

The data

The dependent variable measuring services trade, DSXodst , is taken from the International Trade
and Production Database for Estimation (ITPD-E) (Borchert et al., 2021). The ITPD-E is constructed using administrative data and, therefore, does not include information estimated by any
statistical techniques, unlike other trade data sources such as OECD TiVA or the WTO-OECD
BaTiS. Another advantage of ITPD-E is that it covers many countries based on reported data,
in particular developing countries. This is important as we cover many non-OECD countries in
Asia, Central and Latin America, and Africa. The choice for this database comes, however with
a trade-off as it covers only trade data from 2000 to 2019. Yet, these 20 years represent a period
within which most of the policy changes took place, as illustrated in Figure 1.
To select our list of digital sectors, we use different categories of digital services for a
more nuanced analysis. First, the OECD-WTO-IMF Handbook on Measuring Digital Trade
(OECD-WTO-IMF, 2020) separates digital trade into two (overlapping) categories of sectors, distinguishing between digitally ordered and digitally delivered trade. Because the former is more
difficult to measure, and because the handbook concludes that only services can be digitally delivered, we focus on digitally delivered services. The handbook defines digitally delivered services
as comprising all services except those closely tied to goods trade such as transport, processing of
physical inputs owned by others, maintenance and repair of goods, travel and construction. This
scope of sectors is admittedly broad.
An alternative indicator for assessing sectors as digital is based on the proxy developed by

Data protection regime

A further extension of our baseline model is to assess whether the presence of a comprehensive
data protection regime in both countries has any differential effect on digital services trade. We
do so by creating an interaction variable that multiplies the three dummies for each data model
with another dummy that evaluates whether countries have applied a comprehensive law for data
protection. Our estimated equation becomes as follows:
DSXodst = exp [𝜃1 MODELodst + 𝜃2 MODELodst ∗ Protodst + 𝛿ods + 𝛿odt + 𝛿ost + 𝛿dst ] ∗ 𝜀odst .

(3)

The term MODELodst represents the vector in which the three data models, OPodst , RSodst and
GCodst are covered as outlined in Equation (2). The variable Protodst denotes whether country-pairs
share a comprehensive data protection law. On the one hand, a data protection regime could
increase costs for firms because of compliance; one the other hand it could support trade by
instilling trust or reducing the costs of regulatory heterogeneity. Here too we allow the interaction variable to vary by sector, in addition to country-pair and year. A lag of 2 years is also applied
on the data protection variable.
Table 4 reports the results. The results for the three models remain generally unchanged.
Yet, we find interesting additional findings for the interaction variables. First of all, this dummy
does not show any significant result when interacted with the open model. Hence, the fact that
country-pairs following an open model also share a comprehensive data protection regime does
not appear to affect trade in digital services. Interestingly, however, the interaction variable for the
conditional model with our data protection dummy comes out strongly positive with the coefficient size in all four columns being large and becoming more sizable when broadening the scope
of digital sectors. Therefore, it indicates that the negative effects of the conditional model for
cross-border flows are offset by the presence of a strong data protection law in place domestically.
In column 4 this negative effect is entirely compensated.
The interactions for the government control model are negative and strongly significant with a
large coefficient size. One potential explanation is that when countries implement strict controls
on the transfers of data, consumers lack trust in the digital economy because the government
has a wide degree of discretion in accessing personal data. In this situation, strict controls on
data transfers are usually justified by security and public order rationales. The digital economy
becomes a tool for enabling surveillance and the presence of a comprehensive data protection
law would not be enough to generate trust in the digital economy. As a result, firms would be
left to deal with the additional compliance costs connected with the conditional regime without
benefitting from the trust component derived from this framework.

CO N C LU S I O N

The regulation of cross-border data transfers remains a controversial issue in the international
trade arena, including in the on-going plurilateral discussions on e-commerce at the WTO and
in regional negotiations. Evidence assessing the impact of the different models of regulations of
data remains scarce, particular with respect to personal data. This article aims to fill this gap and
provide evidence to inform the regional and global discussions on data policies.

We use a gravity model to assess if sharing a similar regulatory model between trading partners regarding the cross-border transfer of personal data and having enacted a comprehensive
data protection law is associated with trade in digital services. Rules governing the cross-border
transfer of personal data follow roughly three different regulatory models, each based on different features: one based on open transfers of data, a second based on conditional transfers with
safeguards in place, and a third based on government-controlled transfers.
We expect that this regulatory divergence would distort trade in digital services across the
global economy, but our findings show that this is not always the case. In fact, we find that sharing
an open data model supports trade in digital services, while we find a negative effect of sharing the
conditional model. This finding is in line with the literature that highlights the costs connected
with complying with ambitious data protection regulations like the GRPR. In our analysis, we find
no evidence with respect to the control model, despite previous studies and government surveys
highlighting the high costs connected with local processing requirements. A possible explanation
is that trade in digital services between countries following the control model may not entirely
driven by economic reasons but by political ties.
We also study the interaction of the different models applied to cross-border transfers with
the adoption of a comprehensive data protection law by the country-pair. On the one hand, such
a policy might create additional costs for businesses because of compliance; on the other hand,
it can raise trust for consumers and could reduce regulatory heterogeneity—especially given that
the EU data protection law has been adopted in a considerable number of countries. We find no
differential effect with regards to the open model. However, the interaction for the conditional
model with our data protection dummy comes out strongly positive. This indicates that the negative effects of the conditions attached of this model are muted by the presence of a strong data
protection regime in place domestically. This offsetting effect is large.
Finally, the interactions for the government control model are negative and strongly significant with a large coefficient size. One potential explanation is that when countries implement
strict controls on the transfers of data, citizens would expect a wide degree of discretion in the
access to these data by the government. In these cases, the digital economy becomes a tool to facilitate surveillance and the presence of a comprehensive data protection law would not be enough
to generate trust in the digital economy. As a result, firms would be left to deal with the additional compliance costs connected with the conditional regime without benefitting from the trust
component derived from this framework.
While previous studies found that restrictions on data transfers have a negative impact on
digital services, this study provides more nuanced results for personal data that vary depending
on the type of governance model adopted by the country and the presence of a comprehensive
data protection regime. The latter results indicate that differences in regulatory rules regarding
data can be offset. Therefore, more research is needed on the specific channels through which
such regulatory heterogeneity between countries can be reduced.
